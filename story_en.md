# Author's Note: When an Ordinary Person Stands on the Shoulders of Giants

**By Wang Jing**  
**Date**: 2026-04-04  
**Project**: Orchestra v1.0

---

At this moment, the code of Orchestra v1.0 lies quietly on GitHub. After forty-eight hours of intensive development, I sit here and write these words—not as technical documentation, but as the inner monologue of an ordinary person.

It all started with a late night when I read through the source code of Claude Coordinator. The shock was pure: I, a game producer, actually understood the core design of a global AI leader. But after the shock came a deeper unease: if I just "understand" and leave, how am I different from the thousands of admirers?

So came the question: "Standing on the shoulders of this giant, how far can I go?"

Four days. That's my answer.

In these four days, my AI assistants and I completed a "reconstructive innovation": based on the philosophy of Coordinator, we built 11 core modules from scratch, all 77,000 lines of code handwritten. We enabled over 150 Agents to collaborate and create, outputting 100,000 words of engineering notes. We preset 53 professional game development Agents, but more importantly, we proved that anyone can build their own expert network based on this framework.

But technical details were never the core of this story.

The real core is the relationship between an ordinary person and the times.

There was a time when AI evolution was a "miracle" in the laboratory, a patent of giant corporations. We ordinary people were just "users" and "consumers" of technology. But Orchestra's four-day journey told me: the paradigm has changed.

When open-source code makes the most cutting-edge ideas accessible, when powerful model capabilities can be called on demand, when an enthusiast can build a production-grade system that can coordinate 150+ intelligent agents in just four days—what does this mean?

It means that technological evolution and democratization are happening simultaneously. It means that "pushing AI forward" is no longer exclusive to researchers in white lab coats, but belongs to every ordinary person with ideas and execution.

I often think, what is the true power of open source? Is it the code? No, it's the equal distribution of possibilities. Claude gave the world an exquisite Coordinator, and what Orchestra tries to do is to turn this exquisite design into a tool that anyone can use, into a cornerstone that anyone can continue to build upon.

Giants let us see the scenery at the mountaintop, and what we ordinary people need to do, perhaps, is to make the road wider on its shoulders, to pave the steps more smoothly, so that more people can walk up, see that scenery, and even, see farther.

Orchestra v1.0 is still young. I know that in the grand narrative of AI, this is just a speck of dust. But what moves me is precisely this "smallness"—when countless such "small" advances happen simultaneously, the evolution of technology is no longer linear progress, but the spread of a spark.

The greatest revelation these four days gave me was not a technical breakthrough, but a cognitive liberation: in today's AI wave, everyone can be not just a passenger, but also a sailor, even, someone who can adjust the course of this great ship just a little bit.

So, I open-source all the code. This is not just a technical choice, but an expression of belief: I believe collaboration triumphs over closure, I believe the power of community triumphs over working alone, I believe the best innovations often happen at the boundaries of ideas, in the co-creation of countless ordinary people.

If you've read this far and feel even a hint of curiosity about the world in that GitHub repository, I want to say: go take a look. It's not a perfect masterpiece, it's an answer given by an ordinary person in four days, it's a concrete attempt at "what can we do standing on the shoulders of giants."

And the answer is always in the next question, in the next four days, in the moment when the next person stands on the shoulders of giants.

Giants gave us vision.

And what we need to do is to make this vision a common starting point for everyone.
